## Q2:

  

In extract A, we can see that this young child spends most of his time playing near and in the presence of his parents. For example "switch[ing] off the television", when his dad is watching it or "running up to people", and "sound… that comes to ambush [them]." This presents to the reader that the parents' child is one that spends most of his company with the people he knows and who love him. This presumably means that this child is more likely to have a more extroverted character in comparison with the child in extract B. The child in extract B, tends to play games in a more solitary environment, such as playing with cards, building "card-houses", or playing with "go-karts" and "wooden sheep and cattle". This more distant relationship could also be the reason why the mother resents not spending more time with her boy, and that the boy prefers spending his time alone (or at least more distant from his mother).

  

The children also seem to be different in the medium they use to amuse themselves. The child in extract B prefers using physical objects (toys), whereas the child in extract A prefers enjoying the presence of others, being a "bit of a show-off" which implies he likes the attention he receives from people, being dramatic ("bark[ing] gibberish"), for which I believe further emphasises the extroverted nature of this child. The child in extract B however, seems to be more drawn to physical objects for entertainment, like the previously mentioned go-karts, which implies he prefers his own company and thus behaves in a more (almost antisocial), introverted nature.

  
  
  
  
  
  
  
  
  

## Q3:

  

You now need to refer only to Source A from lines 18 to 28.

How does the writer use language to describe his son?

  

The writer uses a variety of techniques to display how his son, is both wild and untamed, while still being a small child (most likely for the intent of comedic effect).

  

Firstly, the writer uses an array of imagery to describe the energetic (possibly wild and raw)nature of his son's behaviour. He describes how when the boy "gets tired", he begins "bark[ing] gibberish in the middle of the room", firstly the usage of the verb "barking", implies a parallel of dog-like and animalistic traits, showing how his sons behaviour is untamed, and possibly not accustomed to the behaviour expected of society (at an older age of course). The author then continues to describe how "he throws his entire body into [barking], like he’s trying to shove the noise up a hill". The pairing of a metaphor followed by a simile further emphasises this prolonged idea (usage of an extended metaphor if you will), of primal instincts, saying how he is summoning everything in his tiny being to accomplish the goal of getting attention ("throwing his entire body"), the imagery provided next ("trying to shove the noise up a hill"), could draw parallels of a somewhat sisyphean effort from the child, most likely not always being able to accomplish this goal.

  

This is also continued in the next set of lines where he states that his son attempts to lunge and attack (in a playful kind of way evidently) his parents, even though he may not always be successful; this shows once again his primal behaviour ("every sound and reaction ready to ambush us").

  

Interestingly, this is juxtaposed with the obvious fact that he is just a small boy (despite his rapid growth), who is found afraid of some of those taller and older than him ("irrationally terrified of [his grandad]").

  
  
  
  
  
  
  
  
  
  
  
  
  
  
  
  
  
  

## Q4:

For this question, you need to refer to the whole of Source A, together with the

whole of Source B. Compare how the writers convey their different perspectives and feelings about their children growing up.

In your answer, you could:

• compare their different perspectives and feelings

• compare the methods the writers use to convey their different

perspectives and feelings

• support your response with references to both texts.

  
  

First and foremost I would like to mention the biggest difference between the writer's experience with their children growing up, the time frame in which they are written. More specifically, the writer of extract A, is currently taking care of his child, experiencing at that moment the changes that his son develops (and leaves behind); provided that he still is with his son since his son "turned one last week". Whereas in extract B the writer has long not had the experience of their child in their home ("It is a long while now since he disappeared"). This evident difference between the two writers provides two first hand accounts of the children as an almost 'before and after' situation when placed next to one and other. 

  

Interestingly both parents seem to understand how quickly time goes by, despite this quite stark difference in time (relative to the age of their child growing up). However, an intriguing difference mentioned by both is how prepared they were (and will be in the case of the father in extract A) with how they will react when they no longer will be with their children. In the case of the father in extract A has come to terms with the fact that the time with their child will is unfortunately "slipping just beyond [his] grasp", and understands that this is an "important part of the process", of the child growing up. While the parent in extract B, seems to have been unprepared with the unfortunate moment her son grew and left her, and is constantly longing for her son to be her "little boy in a long white night gown, lying in his crib", to hear "a hearty shout, [and] a shrill whistle". This overarching sadness connecting the two extracts is evident, however it seems that despite this, the writer of extract A time and time again brings it back to being lighthearted, adding the occasional humorous hyperbole or and sarcastic tone (such as "I simply mean that I haven’t slept for a year and I don’t really know how time works any more"). 

  

Which is another difference between the two extracts. The tone in which the two are speaking. I have already mentioned that the parent in extract A uses a more friendly and personal tone, with an obvious direct focus on the reader ("you can't hoard time" the usage of a direct 2nd person pronoun you), including himself in this story as its a written with a sort of reflective mind; while the parent in extract B finds themselves writing this extract as if it were directed to herself, it in a sense is unnecessarily personal for being part of a newspaper article (e.g. the constant mention of her guests complementing her home and her complaining as if internally how she misses replacing "lost buttons and obliterate mud stains").

  
  

## Q5:

  

‘Parents today are overprotective. They should let their children take part in

adventurous, even risky, activities to prepare them for later life.’

Write an article for a broadsheet newspaper in which you argue for or against

this statement.

  

As a young child, I remember vividly the long winter nights I would spend with my parents, all cuddled up together under the blanket watching some cartoon or film, or something similar to that. What memories truly stand out for me as a teenager and remain with me, consist of those moments that I had with my family. The paternal and maternal love, that was both unconditional and omnipresent. Sometimes I wonder how some few others do not share my same form of experiences as children, and it's honestly a shame that this is the case, because from my close friends and family they too feel that those moments they felt all those years ago are an unmatched experience. Sure that may be unclear to some parents at first, that these memories will always remain with their children, after the occasional rant from the untamed and brave horse that is the child when he refuses to take photos together or eat every morsel left off their plates until they glimmer in the dining room lights, but these I feel are just momentary specs in the minds of a child (although this might not reflect in a similar way the parents often remembering the difficult and seemingly endless nights awake tending to their kin).

  

As recent as 2017, there have been numerous surveys asking this exact question of if parents are overprotective of their children, and 59% of the people believed that the parents are too restrictive. Unfortunately these results were largely based off adults who were yet to experience the 'joy' of the bringing up of a child, if we were to remove this group of people, the results come just shy of 80% favouring in the opposite direction, believing that this is not the case.
